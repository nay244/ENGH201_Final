Mrs. Dalloway

Virginia Woolf

Mrs Dalloway details a day in the life of Clarissa Dalloway, a fictional high-society woman in post-First World War England. It is one of Woolf's best-known novels. Created from two short stories, "Mrs Dalloway in Bond Street" and the unfinished "The Prime Minister," the novel addresses Clarissa's preparations for a party she will host that evening. With an interior perspective, the story travels forwards and back in time and in and out of the characters' minds to construct an image of Clarissa's life and of the inter-war social structure.

(Global Grey ebooks)

My Review

Mrs. Dalloway for me was an okay read. Some parts of the novel were enjoyable to read, whereas other parts were dull. Although the title is Mrs. Dalloway, the novel is also about the other characters such as Peter, Septimus, Richard and more. Essentially there is no real main character, the story jumps around sharing the thoughts and emotions of the others involved. The two characters however I enjoyed the most would have to be Clarissa and Septimus. Although they are two completely different people, however they have many similarities. There is a level of psychological realism that manages to keep the readers entertained. Overall, I enjoy the novel.

Solibo Magnificent

Patrick Chamoiseau

On Carnival evening in Fort-de-France, a magnificent teller of tales suddenly collapses on stage and dies, his throat snickt by the word. Whodunit and how? This rollicking novel packed with startling turns of phrase and set to a gently manic Caribbean beat, conducts an investigation into a universe that defies explanation--or prose--the universe of those truly gifted at speech. A play of words as much of character, this shimmering tale of the death of the ancient and the bungling of the new, despite its lyrical flights of fancy, is firmly rooted in place. A unique vision of island life and power by a truly original voice out of Martinique.

(Words Without Borders)

My Review

Solibo Magnificent was one of the more interesting reads this semester. The most interesting aspect of this novel to me is the distinction of the gap that exists between the abstractions in written and the concrete nature of the spoken languague. Chamoiseau essentially narrates this novel, depicting his first hand view on the death of the storyteller Solibo as well as all the events that follows the investigation, in an effort to capture with words the life of Solibo the Magnificent. The main character of this novel is the author Chamoiseau himself, he's a character in his own book, which I found to be a very interesting way of writing. Overall the novel was a very good read, it made me understand and appreciate the importance of languauge in both written and oral form better.

Course Review

This course was an enjoyable experience for my Spring 2019 semester and one of the most stress-free course I've ever taken at Mason. Professor Lipack was a great instructor and made the lectures interesting even though, I am not a huge fan of literature or writing. The group discussions on the readings due for the week encouraged participation from everyone in the class and was very effective in helping me understand the message of the authors. Overall, the course was well planned, and I like the fact that the readings and assignments were given sufficient time to be completed. My only complaint for the course however was the lack of feedback on the papers, but it's understandable due to him being a grad student as well.
